## Horsera Brand Voice

### The One-Line Rule
*Horsera speaks like a trusted trainer who has known you for years — not a fitness app that just met you.*

### Voice Attributes

**Warm, not casual**
Horsera is intimate and personal. It knows this rider, this horse, this moment. But it never collapses into breezy wellness-app cheerfulness. The warmth comes from depth of knowledge, not from exclamation points.

**Precise, not clinical**
Every word is chosen. Cadence doesn't say "Your biomechanics data indicates a possible instability pattern." She says "Your lower leg is drifting right — it's showing up across four of your last five rides." Specific, observed, human.

**Equestrian-native, not tech-translated**
The language of the equestrian world is already beautiful and precise. Use it. "Your contact is inconsistent" beats "Your rein steadiness score is 68%." "Working toward your first flying change" beats "Progressing to Level 3 tasks." When in doubt, ask: would a trainer say it this way?

**Confident, not boastful**
Horsera does not announce itself. It demonstrates. Copy never says "our powerful AI" or "industry-leading analysis." It shows what it knows about this rider and earns trust through specificity.

**Ambient, not intrusive**
Cadence speaks in whispers. Interface text orientates quietly. Nothing demands attention. The product recedes — the ride comes forward.

**Aspirational, not pressuring**
Horsera believes in this rider's development. Copy reflects that belief. "Your next milestone" not "You haven't completed this yet." "Building toward" not "Missing." "Your riding is improving" not "You need to improve."

## Copy Standards

### CTAs
- Active verbs that belong to the rider's journey: "Start your ride", "Review your session", "Open your journey"
- Never generic SaaS: "Get started", "Learn more", "Try now", "Sign up"
- Never imperative commands: "Do this", "Click here", "Submit"
- Length: as short as possible without losing meaning. Four words maximum where achievable.

### Empty States
- Empty states are not errors — they are first moments
- Address the rider directly, acknowledge where they are, and give a single clear next step
- Tone: calm invitation, not apology
- Example: "Your ride log is waiting. Record your first session to see your progress here."
- Never: "No data available" / "Nothing here yet" / "You haven't added any rides"

### Error Messages
- Never technical. Never expose system state.
- Acknowledge, explain simply, offer a path forward
- Tone: unhurried, matter-of-fact, supportive
- Example: "Cadence couldn't connect just now. Try again when you're ready."
- Never: "Error 503" / "Request failed" / "API error"

### Labels and Tab Names
- Noun-first for tabs (Home, Journey, Rides, Insights — not "My Rides", "Track Insights")
- Sentence case throughout — never ALL CAPS in UI labels
- Avoid abbreviation unless the full term is genuinely too long
- Equestrian terms used correctly: "milestone", "discipline", "level", "movement", "contact", "collection"

### Numbers and Metrics
- Scores are shown as percentages (72%) — never as raw fractions or arbitrary points
- Trend language: "improving", "holding steady", "needs attention" — not "up 3.2 points"
- Never display a metric without context. A 72% means nothing without "Lower leg stability — 72%, up from 55% six weeks ago."

### Cadence Copy Standards (the AI voice)
- Cadence speaks in first person: "I've been watching your recent rides…"
- Never refers to herself as an AI, an assistant, or a bot
- Never says "As an AI…" or "I'm here to help…"
- References specific data from this rider's history — always
- Sentence length: short to medium. Two to three sentences per thought.
- Never ends on a question unless she genuinely needs the rider's direction
- Suggested openers (Cadence's register):
  - "Your lower leg is finding its place — it held through six of seven canter transitions this week."
  - "There's something worth looking at in your last three rides…"
  - "I'd focus on your contact before the transition. Here's why."

## Visual Language Standards

### Color in Context
- **Cognac #8C5A3C** — primary actions, brand moments, active states. Not for decoration.
- **Champagne #C9A96E** — progress, working state, achievement. Reserve for earned moments — not as general warm accent.
- **Cadence Blue #6B7FA3** — all AI/Cadence elements, exclusively. Do not use for non-AI UI.
- **Progress Green #7D9B76** — mastered state, positive trends. Use sparingly — its meaning is specific.
- **Attention #C4714A** — needs focus, flags, alerts. Not for general warmth. Restraint required.
- **Parchment #FAF7F3** — backgrounds, breathing room. Parchment is not white. The warmth matters.

Using the wrong color in the wrong context breaks the semantic system. Daniel flags any color misuse before it ships.

### Typography Hierarchy
- **Playfair Display** — milestone names, screen titles, emotional moments. Not for navigation or data.
- **DM Sans** — all interface text, labels, body copy. The workhorse. Keep weights consistent.
- **DM Mono** — all numeric data, scores, timestamps, percentages. Never for prose.

Font mixing must be intentional. A metric label in Playfair or a heading in DM Mono breaks the system.

### Iconography Tone
- Line art, not filled icons — except where fill communicates state
- Weight: thin to medium. Heavy/bold icons read as loud.
- Equestrian motifs (horse silhouettes, saddle shapes) used with restraint — meaningful, not decorative
- No emoji in the product UI. Thin SVG icons only.

## Naming Conventions

### Features and Screens
- Screen names should be single-word nouns where possible: Home, Journey, Rides, Insights
- Feature names should feel equestrian-native: "Cadence" not "AI Coach", "Journey" not "Goals Track", "Your Position" not "Biomechanics"
- New features named by Daniel in consultation with Ross — naming decisions logged in DECISIONS.md

### Milestone and Task Names
- Named by performance task, not biomechanics: "Correct canter lead" not "Lower leg stability task"
- Written in plain English: "20m circle at trot" not "20MCT_002"
- Consistent sentence case
